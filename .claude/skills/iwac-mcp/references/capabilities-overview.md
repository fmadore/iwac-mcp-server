# Capabilities Overview — "What can you do?"

Source material for answering capability questions in plain language ("what can you do?", "qu'est-ce que tu peux faire ?", "what's in IWAC?", "how could I search this?"). Answer in the user's language, adapt to anything they've already mentioned, and keep it conversational — do **not** recite this file, enumerate the tools, or launch the research workflow.

## What the collection is

The Islam West Africa Collection (IWAC, <https://islam.zmo.de>) is a curated digital archive documenting Islam and Muslim life in francophone West Africa — Burkina Faso, Côte d'Ivoire, Benin, Togo and Niger (plus Nigerian audiovisual material) — from the 1960s to the 2020s. Where most digitisation projects in African studies focus on manuscripts, IWAC preserves the region's **print culture**: six decades of press coverage, and the publications Muslim communities produced about themselves.

Six kinds of material:

- **~12,300 newspaper articles** (mainstream press, five countries) — a continuous, dated record of Muslim life: religious celebrations, the hajj, mosque inaugurations, association politics, debates on laïcité and Islamic education, women and youth, security.
- **~1,500 Islamic publications** — 25 periodicals produced by associations, mosques and schools (Islam Info, An-Nasr Vendredi, Plume Libre, ASSALAM, Le CERFIste…): the community's own voice, with concerns and theological positions often absent from the mainstream press.
- **~4,700 index records** — curated authority entries for persons, organizations, places, events and subjects, linked to the articles that mention them.
- **864 academic references** — scholarship on Islam in the region, in French and English.
- **26 association documents** (reports, flyers — mostly Burkina Faso) and **45 audiovisual recordings** (Nigeria, incl. Hausa/Arabic).

## Why the press matters (talking points)

- Newspapers provide **fixed chronological anchors** — precise dates, named actors, direct quotations, immediate reactions — where oral sources are vulnerable to memory and loss.
- The collection pairs an **outside view** (journalists observing Muslim communities) with an **inside view** (Islamic publications speaking for themselves).
- Coverage history is itself evidence. Before the late 1980s the press mostly reported festivals, pilgrimages and mosque openings; after political liberalisation (~1990) it documents associations, internal debates, leadership struggles and the growing public presence of Islam.
- The press reveals **power dynamics**: who gains media visibility and who is marginalised — including the contrast between state organs (the only press before ~1991) and the private press that followed.

## What kinds of questions it answers well

- **Religious practice and debate** — Mawlid controversies, Ramadan, hajj organisation with its logistical, financial and political disputes.
- **Islamic diversity** — Sufi orders, the Ahmadiyya, Salafi/Wahhabi currents, intra-community rivalries and reconciliations.
- **State–religion relations** — laïcité, Islamic education and Franco-Arabic schools, family-code reform, political participation.
- **Social questions** — women's rights, veiling, youth activism, begging, interfaith dialogue.
- **Islamic NGOs, finance and humanitarian action** — the associative boom, Islamic banking debates.
- **Community organisation** — federations, imam successions, leadership crises, association life.
- **Transnational connections** — Saudi, Libyan, Iranian, Egyptian, Moroccan and Kuwaiti engagement; the OIC and Islamic Development Bank; the francophone Muslim conference circuit (CIMEF, SIFRAM) — an "Islamic Francophonie".
- **Security** — terrorism, radicalisation, and Muslim communities' responses since the 2010s.

Because reports are date-stamped and location-specific, the collection also supports **following people and networks across borders**: preaching tours, funding streams, educational pathways, diplomatic visits — event-level microhistory that adds up to regional, translocal patterns.

## Ways into the data (recommend 2-3, matched to the question)

- **Keyword search** (French for press and publications; French *and* English for scholarship) across titles, full OCR text and AI abstracts.
- **Curated themes** — 214 subject tags (Hadj, Laïcité, Ramadan, Éducation islamique…) for precise, pre-classified slices.
- **People, organisations, places** — find the canonical name among the 4,700 index records, then pull every article that mentions it.
- **Conceptual (semantic) search** in any language, when keywords would miss paraphrases — "Islamic education reform" also surfaces madrasa-modernisation pieces.
- **Tone and framing** — every article carries AI sentiment labels (polarity, centrality, subjectivity); compare how topics, countries or outlets frame Islam.
- **By outlet and period** — filter by newspaper, country, date range; contrast state vs. private press, pre- vs. post-1990.
- **Inside Islamic periodicals** — browse the 25 series, search tables of contents (complete for 17 smaller magazines), or pull keyword excerpts from a full issue.
- **Scholarship** — connect press findings to the 864 academic references.

## Example questions (adapt, don't recite)

- How did the Burkinabè press cover the organisation of the hajj in the 1980s versus the 2000s?
- Who were the public voices of the Ahmadiyya in Côte d'Ivoire, and how were they framed?
- What debates around laïcité and Islamic schooling appear in the Beninese press?
- How did Muslim associations respond to the fall of Blaise Compaoré in 2014?
- What does Plume Libre reveal about Muslim student activism that the mainstream press misses?
- Which Gulf or North African actors funded mosques and schools in the region, and when?

## Caveat to weave in (one sentence)

The collection is curated rather than exhaustive, ~96% French-language, strongest for Burkina Faso and Côte d'Ivoire, thin for Niger and (for the press) absent for Nigeria — it views Islam mainly through the francophone public sphere, and its pre-1990s layer through state-controlled newspapers.
